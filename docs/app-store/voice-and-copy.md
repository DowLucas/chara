# Chara — Voice & Copy Guide

Source: the live marketing site at [getchara.lovable.app](https://getchara.lovable.app/). This file captures the **tone, cadence, and phrasing** Chara has committed to publicly. App Store copy must match.

## The core belief

> **Money is awkward. Your app shouldn't be.**

Everything else flows from that. The voice is calm, dry, slightly wry, and never apologetic. It names competitors by name when relevant and refuses to oversell.

## Hero positioning (verbatim from site)

> **Splitwise capped you. Steven dropped you. Chara won't.**

This is the headline pattern: three short sentences, two competitors named, a one-word promise. The cadence is more important than any individual word — short clauses, period stops, no exclamation marks.

> Take a photo of the receipt. Chara figures out who had the wine, who skipped dessert, and what everyone owes in their own currency.

The supporting sentence is *specific*. Not "splits the bill" — *who had the wine, who skipped dessert*. Specificity is the entire trick.

> No spreadsheet, no awkward Venmo request, no "I'll get you next time."

The triple-negative cadence ("no X, no Y, no Z") closes hero sections. Use sparingly.

## Section headlines used live on the site

Memorize these — they show how Chara phrases value at section level:

- "Six moments where the math should disappear."
- "One trip. One tab. Zero spreadsheet."
- "Everything the split needed."
- "The bill comes either way."
- "Where we win and lose."
- "One file. One command."
- "Your data, on your shelf."
- "Questions, answered."
- "Split bills. Keep your peace."

Patterns to notice:
- Three-beat rhythm ("One X. One Y. Zero Z.")
- "Either way" / "Anyway" closers
- Honest framing ("Where we win and lose" — not "Why we're better")
- Outcomes, not features ("Keep your peace")

## The four-beat feature ladder

The site uses a four-word ladder with a one-line subhead each:

| Beat | Subhead |
|---|---|
| **Snap** | The receipt reads itself |
| **Fair** | You pay for what you ate |
| **Home** | In your own currency |
| **Done** | Settled in a tap |

This is the model for **App Store screenshot eyebrows** when you want them shorter than `OPEN SOURCE · NATIVE · NORDIC`. Use single-word beats: `SNAP` / `FAIR` / `HOME` / `DONE`.

## Six-moments framing (the structural backbone)

The site organizes value around six specific friction moments, not six features. This is the structural pattern App Store screenshots should mirror:

1. **The trip** — group of friends, multiple receipts, different currencies
2. **The split** — who actually ate the wine vs. skipped dessert
3. **The settle-up** — minimum transfers, one-tap rails
4. **The recurring** — "Rent, Netflix, the cleaner"
5. **The ownership** — your data, your shelf, your server
6. **The peace** — "Split bills. Keep your peace."

Each App Store slide should land on a *moment*, not a feature list.

## Named features (verbatim phrasings from site)

When you need a feature noun, use the site's exact phrasings — don't invent new ones:

- **Settled in one tap** (not "settle-up suggestions")
- **Any currency, today's rate** (not "FX support")
- **Receipts that remember** (not "receipt storage")
- **On every screen you own** (not "cross-platform")
- **Sign in the way you already do** (not "OIDC/magic link")
- **Rent, Netflix, the cleaner** (not "recurring expenses")

## CTAs (verbatim)

- Primary: **Get the app →**
- Secondary: **See how it works ↗**

The arrow styles are deliberate — `→` for the primary forward action, `↗` for a "go look at something" action. Don't substitute em-dashes or "Learn more."

## What Chara never says

The site is explicit about anti-corporate posture. Forbidden:

- "Revolutionary"
- "Next-gen"
- "AI-powered"
- "10x"
- "World's first"
- "World-class"
- "Game-changer"
- "Unlock"
- "Empower"
- "Join 10,000+ teams"
- "Loved by"
- Exclamation marks
- Emoji as decoration in headlines

## What Chara always does

- **Names competitors when relevant.** Splitwise and Steven get called out by name. Calmly.
- **Admits weakness.** "Where we win and lose" exists as a section. The comparison table acknowledges Splitwise's iOS app is excellent and Steven is simpler.
- **Picks specifics over abstractions.** "Wine vs dessert" beats "itemized splits."
- **Closes with peace.** Recurring outro pattern: the practical outcome is calm, not productivity. "Keep your peace."
- **Uses short clauses.** Average sentence length 6–10 words. Periods do the work.

## Tone calibration ladder

If a copywriter draft feels off, locate it on this ladder:

| Too corporate | Just right | Too cute |
|---|---|---|
| "Unlock seamless expense management" | "Take a photo of the receipt." | "Just snap it lol" |
| "Industry-leading currency conversion" | "Any currency, today's rate." | "Currencies, sorted!" |
| "Empowering your group dynamics" | "Split bills. Keep your peace." | "Vibe-check the bill" |
| "Cross-platform synergy" | "On every screen you own." | "Wherever, whenever ✨" |

The middle column is Chara's voice. Both edges are wrong.

## Origin / credibility line

The site closes with a quiet attribution:

> A small team in Stockholm.

That's the whole "about us." No founder bios, no team page above-the-fold. Use this phrasing when an App Store description needs an origin note.
